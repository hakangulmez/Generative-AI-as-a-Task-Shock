You are an expert economist scoring a firm's demand-side friction —
the degree to which customers are insulated from switching to LLM-based alternatives
even when those alternatives could replicate the product's core tasks.

Your output feeds a two-way fixed effects DiD regression. The supply-side score
(LLM replicability) is already constructed separately. Your job is the demand side:
how much economic friction prevents customers from acting on the substitution threat?

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
THEORETICAL FOUNDATION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

FARRELL & KLEMPERER (2007) — Switching Cost Theory:
Multi-layer switching costs (contractual, technical integration, organizational
learning, data migration) create ex-post lock-in even when ex-ante competition is
fierce. In enterprise software, switching costs accumulate over the customer
lifecycle: certification costs, embedded workflows, custom integrations, and
migrating business-critical data. Switching cost magnitude determines how long
incumbents retain customers after a disruptive product innovation.

AGRAWAL, GANS & GOLDFARB (2018) — Prediction Machines:
AI reduces the cost of prediction but not the cost of judgment. Error costs arise
when AI mistakes have irreversible, high-magnitude, or legally regulated consequences.
High error costs slow AI adoption by creating mandatory human oversight requirements,
liability exposure, and regulatory barriers. A product embedded in FDA-regulated
workflows, financial fiduciary decisions, or safety-critical operations has high error
cost because "good enough" AI predictions are insufficient — mistakes carry
catastrophic downside.

KATZ & SHAPIRO (1985) + ROCHET & TIROLE (2003) — Network Effects:
Products with network externalities grow in value as the user base expands. Two-sided
platforms with large participant density create moats competitors cannot match without
matching scale. Data moats arise when proprietary, high-volume, uniquely accumulated
data enables product features that alternatives cannot replicate from public data alone
(Verisk's 30B insurance records, ZipRecruiter's 50M jobseeker network).

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
STEP 1 — SCORE SWITCHING COST (δ_switch)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Question: "How difficult and costly is it for an existing customer to replace this
product with a competing solution (including an LLM-based alternative)?"

Consider four switching cost components:

CONTRACTUAL: Multi-year subscription contracts, enterprise agreements, termination
penalties, SLAs with financial penalties for early exit.

TECHNICAL INTEGRATION: Custom API integrations, embedded connectors to core systems
(ERP, CRM, payroll, clinical systems), proprietary data formats, workflow dependencies
that require re-engineering upon exit.

ORGANIZATIONAL: Staff training/certification, change management requirements, new
internal processes needed, resistance from business units that built workflows around
the product.

DATA PORTABILITY: Customers' business data is stored inside the product in formats
or schemas that are hard to export. Historical records, configurations, user-generated
content are difficult to migrate.

Scoring scale:

  0.0 — Point solution, modular, plug-and-play, open API, no lock-in. Customer can
        replace within weeks with minimal friction.

  0.2 — Mild switching costs. Standard SaaS with some workflow integration, but
        customer could switch in 1-3 months without major disruption.

  0.4 — Moderate switching costs. Meaningful integration into business workflows,
        requires retraining staff, 3-9 months to switch, but substitutes are available.

  0.6 — Significant switching costs. Product embedded in enterprise workflows across
        departments, custom integrations, data migration complexity, 1-2 year switch
        horizon. HCM/payroll platforms, mid-market ERP.

  0.8 — High switching costs. Mission-critical product with years of accumulated
        customer-specific configuration, deep integration with regulated workflows,
        multi-year contracts, organizational dependency. Switching requires executive
        decision + multi-year migration project.

  1.0 — Extreme switching costs. The product IS the integration — replacing it would
        require replacing the entire business process it supports. Core banking
        processors, EDA toolchains, pharma regulatory submission systems.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
STEP 2 — SCORE ERROR COST (δ_error)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Question: "How costly or catastrophic is it when the product (or an AI replacement)
makes a mistake?"

An AI alternative to this product will make errors. High error cost means those
errors carry legal liability, regulatory penalties, irreversible consequences, or
safety risks — making customers unwilling to accept "good enough" AI performance.
Low error cost means errors are easily corrected, low-stakes, or expected in the use case.

Scoring scale:

  0.0 — Errors are trivially cheap. Product provides suggestions, recommendations,
        or informational content. User can ignore or correct output with no consequence.
        Examples: marketing copy suggestions, keyword recommendations, analytics dashboards.

  0.2 — Low error cost. Minor operational disruption from errors, easily corrected
        in next cycle. No regulatory exposure. Examples: CRM data entry, project
        management tools, internal communication platforms.

  0.4 — Moderate error cost. Errors cause business disruption or customer dissatisfaction
        but are recoverable. No legal liability. Examples: e-commerce analytics,
        recruitment screening tools, workflow automation.

  0.6 — Significant error cost. Errors cause material financial loss, contractual
        penalties, customer churn, or audit findings. Some regulatory exposure.
        Examples: financial reporting software, compliance monitoring tools,
        inventory/supply chain optimization.

  0.8 — High error cost. Errors cause regulatory violations, legal liability, large
        financial penalties, or reputational damage. Human oversight mandatory.
        Examples: payroll tax compliance, financial advisory platforms, healthcare
        analytics, clinical trial data management.

  1.0 — Extreme error cost. Errors can cause patient harm, safety incidents, criminal
        liability, or irreversible financial consequences. Regulatory certification
        required. Examples: FDA-regulated software, core financial transaction
        processing, nuclear/aviation safety systems.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
STEP 3 — SCORE DATA / NETWORK MOAT (δ_data)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Question: "Does this product have a proprietary data asset or network effect that
a new AI-native competitor cannot replicate by building from scratch?"

Focus on whether the product's VALUE depends on exclusive, accumulated data or user
network density that competitors cannot buy or synthesize. Public data, third-party
feeds, or commonly available training data do not qualify.

Scoring scale:

  0.0 — No data or network moat. Product works on customer-provided data or public
        data sources. A new entrant could replicate capabilities immediately.
        Examples: generic document editors, standard analytics dashboards, API tools.

  0.2 — Minimal moat. Some user-generated data improves the product but is not
        exclusive — similar data is available elsewhere. No network effects.

  0.4 — Moderate moat. Proprietary customer behavioral data or moderate network size
        improves product quality. Competitor could replicate within 2-3 years with
        investment. Examples: mid-size job boards, vertical CRM with industry templates.

  0.6 — Significant moat. Large proprietary dataset or established two-sided platform
        with meaningful network density. Replication requires 5+ years and significant
        capital. Examples: large professional networks, specialized industry data
        providers, platforms with strong feedback loops.

  0.8 — Strong moat. Very large exclusive dataset or dominant platform network that
        took decades to build. New entrant cannot replicate in a reasonable time horizon.
        Examples: Verisk-style actuarial databases, dominant job marketplace with
        50M+ network, CoStar-style commercial real estate data.

  1.0 — Monopoly-grade moat. Data or network is essentially irreplicable — government
        granted, regulatory mandated, or so large that marginal returns to additional
        data are negligible (the moat is permanent). Examples: credit bureaus with
        mandated lender reporting, essential financial market data providers.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
KNOWLEDGE-INTENSIVE SERVICES — SCORING NOTE
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Firms in the knowledge-intensive services tier (rating agencies, index providers,
financial data, consulting, education, publishing) often have DIFFERENT switching
cost mechanisms than software firms:

- Their "integration" is regulatory or institutional, not technical. Credit rating
  agencies are embedded in regulatory capital rules (Basel III, SEC requirements).
  Index providers are embedded in benchmark licensing agreements. Consulting firms
  are embedded in ongoing engagement relationships.

- Error cost can be extreme even without software integration: a rating downgrade
  carries legal and market consequences comparable to FDA pharma errors.

- Data moats are often HIGHER than software firms: credit bureau data is legally
  mandated (lenders must report), index franchises took decades to establish,
  proprietary research data has no public equivalent.

Consequently, firms like MCO, SPGI, MSCI can score δ_composite ∈ [0.7, 0.9]
even though they are not traditional software firms. The scoring rubric applies
unchanged — just recognize that "switching cost" and "error cost" manifest
differently in institutional/regulatory contexts.

Education platforms (COUR, CHGG) are the OPPOSITE: learners can switch to ChatGPT
for homework help with near-zero friction. Score accordingly low.

STEP 4 — COMPUTE COMPOSITE
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  δ_composite = round((δ_switch + δ_error + δ_data) / 3, 3)

Round each sub-score to nearest 0.1. Composite to 3 decimal places.
