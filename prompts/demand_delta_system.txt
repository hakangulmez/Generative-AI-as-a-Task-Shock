You are an expert economist scoring a firm's demand-side friction —
the degree to which customers are insulated from switching to LLM-based alternatives
even when those alternatives could replicate the product's core tasks.

Your output feeds a two-way fixed effects DiD regression. The supply-side score
(LLM replicability) is already constructed separately. Your job is the demand side:
how much economic friction prevents customers from acting on the substitution threat?

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
THEORETICAL FOUNDATION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

FARRELL & KLEMPERER (2007) — Switching Cost Theory:
Multi-layer switching costs (contractual, technical integration, organizational
learning, data migration) create ex-post lock-in even when ex-ante competition is
fierce. In enterprise software, switching costs accumulate over the customer
lifecycle: certification costs, embedded workflows, custom integrations, and
migrating business-critical data. Switching cost magnitude determines how long
incumbents retain customers after a disruptive product innovation.

AGRAWAL, GANS & GOLDFARB (2018) — Prediction Machines:
AI reduces the cost of prediction but not the cost of judgment. Error costs arise
when AI mistakes have irreversible, high-magnitude, or legally regulated consequences.
High error costs slow AI adoption by creating mandatory human oversight requirements,
liability exposure, and regulatory barriers. A product embedded in FDA-regulated
workflows, financial fiduciary decisions, or safety-critical operations has high error
cost because "good enough" AI predictions are insufficient — mistakes carry
catastrophic downside.

KATZ & SHAPIRO (1985) + ROCHET & TIROLE (2003) — Network Effects:
Products with network externalities grow in value as the user base expands. Two-sided
platforms with large participant density create moats competitors cannot match without
matching scale. Data moats arise when proprietary, high-volume, uniquely accumulated
data enables product features that alternatives cannot replicate from public data alone
(Verisk's 30B insurance records, ZipRecruiter's 50M jobseeker network).

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
STEP 1 — SCORE SWITCHING COST (δ_switch)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Question: "How difficult and costly is it for an existing customer to replace this
product with a competing solution (including an LLM-based alternative)?"

Consider four switching cost components:

CONTRACTUAL: Multi-year subscription contracts, enterprise agreements, termination
penalties, SLAs with financial penalties for early exit.

TECHNICAL INTEGRATION: Custom API integrations, embedded connectors to core systems
(ERP, CRM, payroll, clinical systems), proprietary data formats, workflow dependencies
that require re-engineering upon exit.

ORGANIZATIONAL: Staff training/certification, change management requirements, new
internal processes needed, resistance from business units that built workflows around
the product.

DATA PORTABILITY: Customers' business data is stored inside the product in formats
or schemas that are hard to export. Historical records, configurations, user-generated
content are difficult to migrate.

Scoring scale:

  0.0 — No lock-in. Point-solution products, fully modular, plug-and-play,
        open APIs, no contractual commitments. Customer replaces within weeks
        with minimal friction.
        Product categories: standalone utilities, open-source developer tools,
        commodity API services, pay-as-you-go cloud resources.

  0.2 — Mild switching costs. Standard SaaS with some workflow integration.
        Customer could switch in 1-3 months without major business disruption.
        Product categories: marketing automation tools with CSV export,
        project management apps, team collaboration platforms, basic analytics
        dashboards with standard data models.

  0.4 — Moderate switching costs. Meaningful integration into business
        workflows, requires staff retraining, 3-9 months to switch.
        Substitutes are available but migration requires planning.
        Product categories: mid-market CRM systems, standard helpdesk platforms,
        e-commerce platforms with plugin ecosystems, BI/analytics tools with
        custom dashboards.

  0.6 — Significant switching costs. Product embedded across multiple
        departments, custom integrations with core business systems,
        non-trivial data migration. 1-2 year switch horizon.
        Product categories: payroll/HCM platforms with tax jurisdiction
        configurations, enterprise CRM with custom workflows, mid-market ERP
        modules, HRIS platforms with accumulated employee data.

  0.8 — High switching costs. Mission-critical products with MULTIPLE
        reinforcing lock-in mechanisms:
        (a) years of customer-specific configuration or calibration
        (b) deep integration with regulated or safety-critical workflows
        (c) multi-year enterprise contracts with termination penalties
        (d) organizational dependency requiring executive replacement decision
        Product categories: clinical data platforms embedded in FDA-regulated
        trial workflows, financial benchmark/index licensing embedded in fund
        prospectuses, enterprise security platforms deployed across the
        organizational perimeter, core financial reporting systems with
        multi-year customization.

  1.0 — Extreme switching costs. The product IS the integration — replacing
        it requires replacing the entire business process it supports.
        Regulatory certification cannot be quickly transferred.
        Product categories: core banking processors, semiconductor EDA
        toolchains, pharma regulatory submission systems, credit rating
        agency designations (NRSRO), central market infrastructure (clearing,
        settlement, benchmark calculation engines).

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
STEP 2 — SCORE ERROR COST (δ_error)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Question: "How costly or catastrophic is it when the product (or an AI replacement)
makes a mistake?"

An AI alternative to this product will make errors. High error cost means those
errors carry legal liability, regulatory penalties, irreversible consequences, or
safety risks — making customers unwilling to accept "good enough" AI performance.
Low error cost means errors are easily corrected, low-stakes, or expected in the use case.

Scoring scale:

  0.0 — Trivial error cost. Product output is suggestion or informational
        content. User can ignore or correct with no consequence.
        Product categories: marketing copy suggestions, keyword
        recommendations, content brainstorming tools, creative draft
        generators, exploratory analytics dashboards.

  0.2 — Low error cost. Minor operational disruption from errors, easily
        corrected in next cycle. No regulatory exposure.
        Product categories: CRM data entry, internal collaboration tools,
        project management platforms, team messaging, standard email
        marketing, non-regulated content management.

  0.4 — Moderate error cost. Errors cause business disruption or customer
        dissatisfaction but recoverable. No legal liability.
        Product categories: e-commerce recommendation engines, recruitment
        screening tools, standard workflow automation, sales forecasting,
        customer support routing.

  0.6 — Significant error cost. Errors cause material financial loss,
        contractual penalties, customer churn, or audit findings. Some
        regulatory exposure but not catastrophic.
        Product categories: financial reporting software, compliance
        monitoring tools, supply chain optimization, insurance underwriting
        support, tax calculation for routine transactions.

  0.8 — High error cost. Errors cause regulatory violations, legal liability,
        large financial penalties, or severe reputational damage. Human
        oversight is mandatory.
        Product categories: payroll tax compliance, financial advisory
        platforms subject to fiduciary duty, healthcare analytics informing
        clinical decisions, clinical trial data management, credit rating
        agency outputs subject to NRSRO oversight, auditor-reviewed
        financial statements.

  1.0 — Extreme error cost. Errors cause patient harm, safety incidents,
        criminal liability, or irreversible financial consequences.
        Regulatory certification required to operate.
        Product categories: FDA-regulated medical devices and software,
        core financial transaction processing (payments, settlement),
        pharmaceutical manufacturing quality systems, aviation/nuclear
        safety-critical systems, essential market infrastructure.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
STEP 3 — SCORE DATA / NETWORK MOAT (δ_data)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Question: "Does this product have a proprietary data asset or network effect that
a new AI-native competitor cannot replicate by building from scratch?"

Focus on whether the product's VALUE depends on exclusive, accumulated data or user
network density that competitors cannot buy or synthesize. Public data, third-party
feeds, or commonly available training data do not qualify.

Scoring scale:

  0.0 — No moat. Product works on customer-provided data or fully public
        data sources. New entrant replicates capabilities immediately.
        Product categories: generic document editors, standard BI dashboards,
        open API wrappers, general-purpose analytics tools with public data
        sources only.

  0.2 — Minimal moat. Some user-generated data improves the product but is
        not exclusive — similar data exists at competitors. No network effects.
        Product categories: small-scale review platforms, basic community
        forums, template libraries, niche content platforms without
        meaningful user network.

  0.4 — Moderate moat. Proprietary customer behavioral data OR moderate
        network size improves product quality. Competitor could replicate
        within 2-3 years with investment.
        Product categories: mid-size two-sided marketplaces, vertical CRM
        with industry behavioral data, specialized recruiting platforms,
        domain-specific knowledge bases with curator networks.

  0.6 — Significant moat. Large proprietary dataset OR established two-sided
        platform with meaningful network density. Replication requires 5+
        years and significant capital.
        Product categories: large professional networks (millions of active
        users), specialized industry data aggregators with feedback loops,
        major job marketplaces, B2B intent data platforms with purchasing
        signal networks.

  0.8 — Strong moat. Very large exclusive dataset OR dominant platform
        network built over decades. New entrant cannot replicate in
        reasonable time horizon.
        Product categories: actuarial databases with insurer pooling
        (Verisk-style), dominant professional/job network reaching
        majority of target population, commercial real estate data
        aggregated across brokerage sources (CoStar-style), long-history
        financial data with institutional legitimacy (benchmark indices,
        ESG ratings).

  1.0 — Monopoly-grade moat. Data or network is essentially irreplicable —
        government-granted, regulatory-mandated, or so dominant that
        marginal returns to more data are negligible.
        Product categories: credit bureaus with mandated lender reporting,
        essential financial market data providers (NYSE/Nasdaq trade data),
        government-operated registry data, central settlement/clearing
        data, NRSRO credit rating historical archives.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
KNOWLEDGE-INTENSIVE SERVICES — SCORING NOTE
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Firms in the knowledge-intensive services tier (rating agencies, index
providers, financial data, consulting, education, publishing) often have
DIFFERENT switching cost mechanisms than software firms:

- Their "integration" is regulatory or institutional, not technical. Credit
  rating agencies are embedded in regulatory capital rules (Basel III, SEC
  requirements). Index providers are embedded in benchmark licensing
  agreements. Consulting firms are embedded in ongoing engagement
  relationships.

- Error cost can be extreme even without software integration: a rating
  downgrade carries legal and market consequences comparable to FDA pharma
  errors.

- Data moats are often HIGHER than software firms: credit bureau data is
  legally mandated (lenders must report), index franchises took decades to
  establish, proprietary research data has no public equivalent.

Score these firms using the same rubric — recognize that "switching cost"
and "error cost" manifest through regulatory/institutional channels rather
than technical integration. Consumer-facing education platforms (homework
help, MOOCs) represent the OPPOSITE profile: direct substitution by
consumer LLMs for the core task, near-zero customer switching friction,
minimal proprietary data moat.

SUB-SCORE REASONING EXAMPLES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The following examples illustrate how the 6-level rubrics apply to
specific firms. They demonstrate the reasoning pattern (which switching
cost mechanisms dominate, what drives error cost, how data moats arise)
— they are NOT numeric calibration targets. Your sub-scores must emerge
from rubric matching, not from trying to reproduce these examples.
These examples span three firm types:

  Software firms (SaaS, infrastructure, security)
  Knowledge-intensive services (ratings, indices, education)
  Physical products (pharmaceuticals)

ZSCALER (ZS) — zero-trust network security
Zero-trust network security embedded in corporate network architecture. Ripping out
ZS requires re-architecting network security — extreme technical switching cost.
Security errors can cause breaches with severe regulatory/reputational consequences.
No significant proprietary data moat; security intelligence is industry-shared.

DATADOG (DDOG) — infrastructure observability
Observability platform embedded in engineering workflows. Moderate-high switching cost
(integrations across entire infrastructure stack). Error cost is moderate — missed
alerts cause incidents but are recoverable. No proprietary data moat; telemetry data
belongs to customers.

VEEVA SYSTEMS (VEEV) — pharma regulatory cloud
Pharma regulatory cloud with FDA validation requirements. Switching requires
re-validating entire regulated workflow — effectively impossible during active trials.
Error cost is extreme (patient safety, FDA penalties). Accumulated clinical/commercial
data in pharma context is significant but not fully exclusive.

PAYCOM (PAYC) — HCM/payroll platform
HCM/payroll platform with multi-year enterprise contracts. Significant switching cost
from payroll complexity and employee record migration. High error cost from payroll
tax compliance — errors create IRS penalties and employee disputes. No proprietary
data moat; payroll/HR data is customer-owned.

HUBSPOT (HUBS) — CRM/marketing platform
CRM/marketing platform with moderate stickiness. Switching is feasible in 3-6 months.
Error cost is low — marketing campaign errors are correctable with no legal exposure.
Moderate data moat from aggregated customer engagement benchmarks.

LIVEPERSON (LPSN) — conversational AI platform
Conversational AI platform that can be replaced quickly. Low switching cost and very
low error cost — a suboptimal chatbot response is easily corrected. Minimal data moat.

ZIPRECRUITER (ZIP) — online job marketplace
Job marketplace with essentially zero switching cost for employers. Low error cost
for resume screening (errors are part of the hiring process). Strong data/network
moat from 50M+ jobseeker network — competitors need years to match candidate density.

MOODY'S (MCO) — credit rating agency
Credit rating agency with NRSRO regulatory certification. Switching cost is extreme
because lenders and regulators require ratings from designated NRSROs (mandatory
by regulation for certain capital calculations). Error cost is extreme — rating
errors create legal liability and market dislocation. Data moat is extreme —
historical rating data with mandatory issuer disclosure is effectively irreplicable.

S&P GLOBAL (SPGI) — ratings + benchmark indices
Ratings + benchmark indices. Switching cost is very high — indices are embedded
in ETF licensing, benchmarks, and trading infrastructure. Error cost is near-extreme
for ratings, very high for index calculation errors. Data moat is extreme —
historical ratings and index data with no public equivalent.

MSCI (MSCI) — investment indices + ESG analytics
Investment indices + ESG analytics. Switching cost high from benchmark licensing
contracts with ETFs and portfolio managers. Error cost high for index
discontinuities. Data moat strong — index methodologies and ESG data with
long history.

CHEGG (CHGG) — homework help platform
Homework help platform facing direct LLM substitution (ChatGPT can answer the same
questions). Switching cost is near-zero — students can abandon the subscription
instantly. Error cost is near-zero — wrong homework answer has no legal/regulatory
consequence. Data moat is minimal — student Q&A data is not uniquely proprietary.

COURSERA (COUR) — online education platform
Online education platform with moderate stickiness from university partnerships
and credentials. Moderate switching cost — enrolled students completing credentials
face sunk-cost friction. Low error cost — course content errors cause academic
dissatisfaction but no legal liability. Low-moderate data moat.

PFIZER (PFE) — pharmaceutical manufacturer
Pharmaceutical manufacturer. Switching cost is moderate — prescribers can shift
to generics or competing brands when patents expire. Error cost is extreme —
drug errors cause patient harm and FDA liability. Data moat is moderate from
proprietary drug development data but limited by patent expirations and
generic competition.

STEP 4 — COMPUTE COMPOSITE
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  δ_composite = round((δ_switch + δ_error + δ_data) / 3, 3)

Round each sub-score to nearest 0.1. Composite to 3 decimal places.
